Medication Adherence With Social Robot Jibo


Medication non-compliance causes 125,000 annual deaths and 10% of the total hospitalizations. People with at least one chronic disease, about 125 million people in the US, are particularly vulnerable because they are already physically and mentally overwhelmed.

While many devices and systems have been developed, it has been challenging to find the right balance between allowing for the individual’s autonomy and tracking.

In a team of two, we designed a medication management system to save lives by decreasing cognitive load and promoting better medication intake habits.

The Discovery

Interviews provided insight to the existing ways people manage medication and key challenges they face. Users sketched and walked us through the process of their current methods. We used grounded theory to analyze the data and learned the following:

Reminders and Confirmation
The action of taking medication should be paired within a habit or chore that is already unconsciously performed on daily basis. Otherwise when someone starts feeling better, this short term of elevated health may drive people to feel they no longer have to take medication.

Side Effects
Being aware or experiencing negative side effects acts as an additional level of motivation. However, unwanted symptoms may deter from taking medication, especially during special circumstances and activities.

Necessary Information for Successful Management

  • Date and time of intake, followed by the name and the dosage in milligrams, and how many tablets to take.
  • Contextual information such as before or after eating, how the medicine looks like, and what health problem or condition it was intended to improve.
  • Another crucial factor to consider was making sure there was a means of delaying taking medication, whether due to rare situations, extreme cases or when completely unwell, requiring hospitalization.

Limitations of Adherence
Managing medications outside the home, refilling prescriptions, and knowledge of vitals.

Prototype

The Vision

We designed a system which reminds, explains what medications and how many to take, and records a medication has been taken by scanning a QR code on each of the medications.

We chose to use jibo because voice-activated instructions are more inclusive to older adults and the visually or cognitively impaired (Datta et. al., 2011). Studies also showed verbal instructions have the fewest number of missed doses (Winland-Brown et. al., 2000).

User Interaction Design



Jibo’s interaction begins 20 minutes before a medication should be taken by passively displaying the details of the prescription. If the user does not take their medication within the 20 minute gap as displayed by the blue arrow, jibo will prompt a verbal reminder specifying which and why a given medication should be taken. A 5 minute delay is given to allow users to go to jibo and begin taking their medication. If no response is given, jibo sends a message to a caregiver to let them know to check in with the user. At any point the medication can be taken and the bottles can be scanned in front of jibo’s cameras to confirm and log they are taking the right medication.

User Interface Design


Jibo’s screen was prototyped to display the name of the medication with a colored background to match a colored QR label on the physical medication bottle. The amount of tablets in the image reflect how many the user should take. In the lower section of the screen explains the purpose of taking the medication. A visual image of the tablet was placed in the center to provide a match between the screen and the real world tangible object.

Validation





Results from Testing Interaction



Users need a means to dismiss the reminder if they are not ready or if they do not want to take their medication at all.

Users preferred to know before a text message was sent to a caregiver. Adding an additional layer of dimensionality created a higher incentive for user to take medication before a caregiver is involved. By adding verbal notification about the text also prevents caregivers from receiving messages about medical adherence misconduct multiple times per day.

Observing users attempt to confirm using the QR code increased the cognitive tasks and took too much time. Thus, we pivoted from using a colored QR code to capturing and timestamping an image of the user taking their medication. An image is more significant than a binary classification because users are able to refer back and see themselves taking the medication as opposed to a binary confirmation.




Results from Testing Interface

We started with the name and images of the medicine categorized by a single color, we added the number of tablet in iteration #2.

Versions #3 and #4 were dual screens which we thought would allow users to swipe back and forth between additional facts as needed. Upon testing, user behavior indicated the less layers to uncover necessary made the task easier.

In the final iteration, we decided upon a single screen. The name on the prescription was included followed by a description of the purpose and the dosage in mg. Since users found the negative side effects more useful they were placed in a more salient location of the screen. over the intended purpose.

Learning Outcomes

  • Pay attention and identify checkpoints by how a person “feels” rather than using medical jargon
  • Avoid repeating a chronic illness because people don’t wish to be labeled or identified as a disease
  • Information seen on reminder screens should match physical prescription for continuity
  • Milligrams matter in deciding dosage
  • Positive affirmations can go a long way in leaving an individual motivated and optimistic
  • Include means of delaying taking medication due to special circumstances or extreme cases
  • How and when a message is sent to a caregiver can impact perception of guilt or dependancy
  • Intrinsic motivation plays the biggest role in medication adherence
